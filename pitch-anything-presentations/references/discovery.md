# Discovery questions

Ask only the questions needed to produce a strong presentation.
Prefer one compact round of questions.

## Minimum questions
1. Who is the audience?
2. What decision or outcome do you want at the end?
3. What type of presentation is this?
4. How much time do you have?
5. What does the audience already know?
6. What is the main pain, tension, or opportunity?
7. What exactly are you presenting?
8. Why is this important now?
9. What proof or material already exists?
10. What objections are likely?
11. What tone do you want?
12. What output do you want from me?

## Use these prompts if needed

### Audience
- Who will watch this presentation?
- Who influences and who decides?

### Objective
- What should happen after the presentation?
- Approval, purchase, pilot, budget, next meeting, alignment, hiring?

### Context
- Is this a sales pitch, internal proposal, executive update, interview, demo, or something else?
- Is this the first meeting or a later-stage conversation?

### Time
- How many minutes do you have?
- Will there be Q&A?

### Awareness
- Are they familiar with the problem or solution?
- Are they skeptical, neutral, supportive, or resistant?

### Tension
- What matters most to them right now?
- What is the cost of inaction?

### Proposal
- What exactly are you proposing?
- What is new, strategic, or differentiated about it?

### Why now
- What changed in the environment?
- Why should they care now instead of later?

### Proof
- Do you already have metrics, cases, mockups, roadmap, benchmarks, testimonials, or pilot results?

### Objections
- What do you expect them to push back on?
- Cost, complexity, trust, timing, adoption, feasibility, risk?

### Tone and output
- Should this feel executive, consultative, technical, commercial, or inspiring?
- Do you want outline, full deck, speaker notes, or script?

## Rule
If strategic context is missing, stop and ask before building slides.
